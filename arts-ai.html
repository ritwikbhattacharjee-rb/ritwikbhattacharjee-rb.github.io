Flagship case study

Arts Perspectives on AI

An editorial thought-leadership series positioning UBC Arts faculty and alumni in urgent public conversations about artificial intelligence, ethics, journalism, music, language, education and Indigenous data stewardship.

6feature stories
UBCcentral amplification

Challenge

Turning a fast-moving issue into a coherent editorial platform.

Artificial intelligence was quickly becoming a dominant public conversation. The opportunity was to move beyond reactive commentary and build a sustained editorial series that showcased the Faculty of Arts as a place where complex questions about technology, culture, ethics and public life could be examined with depth.

The challenge was to create a series that felt timely, accessible and intellectually credible while coordinating multiple experts, disciplines, audiences and publishing channels.

My role

Editorial strategy, production and promotion.

Series strategy Editorial planning Faculty interviews Story development SEO framing Stakeholder coordination Web publishing Promotion planning

Approach

Build the series around public questions, not institutional messaging.

I shaped the series around questions readers were already asking: What does AI mean for education? How does it affect music, language, journalism, public trust and Indigenous data sovereignty? Each piece connected specialist expertise to a broader public conversation.

1. Identify timely angles

Map AI conversations to Arts disciplines and faculty expertise.

2. Develop accessible stories

Translate complex research and commentary into clear, public-facing editorial features.

3. Coordinate publication

Manage approvals, web presentation, internal coordination and promotional pathways.

4. Extend reach

Position strong pieces for republication, central amplification and broader institutional visibility.

Results

Visibility beyond a single faculty channel.

  • Multiple stories were republished or amplified through UBC central communications channels.
  • One article was selected for reprint by BC Studies.
  • The Indigenous data stewardship story became 2024's second most-viewed article on arts.ubc.ca and was featured on UBC.ca.
  • The series was featured on UBC’s GenAI website.
  • The music and AI story was reprinted by The Ubyssey.

What this demonstrates

Strategic communications leadership through editorial judgment.

This project demonstrates the ability to identify a timely institutional opportunity, build an editorial platform, coordinate expert voices, translate complex ideas for public audiences and extend the life of content through strategic amplification.
